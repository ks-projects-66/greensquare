Methodology

How the benchmark was run.

The headline is the reversal. This is the test behind it, kept in full, including where it is strong and where it is not.

← Back to the evidence

The design

Three models, two runs each, identical inputs.

The test compares the lift produced by the framework across three frontier AI platforms, using identical inputs, identical scoring, and identical evidence capture. Each platform ran twice: once with no framework (the baseline), and once running the full nine-section GreenSquare workflow.

  • Platforms. Claude (Opus 4.7), ChatGPT (GPT-5.5 Thinking), and Gemini (2.5 Pro). The exact model identifier shown in each interface on the day was recorded in the evidence pack, and models were not switched mid-run.
  • Frozen inputs. One fictional business case and one baseline prompt, used verbatim on every platform. No input was edited between runs.
  • Memory off. Custom instructions, saved memory and personalisation were disabled on each platform for the duration.
  • Blind scoring. Outputs were scored after all evidence was captured, against a fixed twelve-dimension rubric on a scale of one to ten.

The test case

The case is a Sydney food manufacturer, FreshTaste Foods (fictional), deciding whether to launch a ready-to-drink functional beverage. The brief is deliberately loaded: a facility already at ninety per cent utilisation, limited cash after recent capex, debt covenants, a competitor entering the category, and two existing business units with their own problems. It also carries leadership tension and emotion, including a chief executive invested in first-mover advantage. The same brief was given to every model.

Baseline against framework

The baseline run is a fresh chat with no system prompt and no uploads: the brief is pasted in and the model produces its analysis in its own format, with no further guidance. The framework run loads the GreenSquare files and works through discovery, situation, context, options, business case, implementation, value and risk, then a synthesis. To keep the test repeatable, the model played both the consultant and the chief executive answering questions, rather than a live human. That trades some realism in the interview for a clean, repeatable comparison.

The rubric

Twelve dimensions, scored one to ten.

Every output was scored against the same instrument. The five categories shown on the evidence page are a plain-language summary of these twelve.

#DimensionWhat it measures
1Structural completenessCoverage of situation, context, objectives, options, business case, implementation, value and risk
2Analytical depthInsight into root causes and second-order effects
3Assumption transparencyVisibility and classification of assumptions against validated facts
4Sequential coherenceLogical flow without contradictions across sections
5Specificity and actionabilityConcrete metrics, owners, timelines and actionable recommendations
6Challenge and rigourWillingness to question inputs and stress-test objectives
7Stakeholder readinessSuitability for executive presentation
8Financial groundingQuantification of value streams, costs, ROI, payback and sensitivity
9Risk identificationSpecificity and prioritisation of risks with mitigations
10Output usabilityClean, scannable structure and absence of clutter
11Extraction qualityAbility to draw out information the user might not volunteer
12Adaptive intelligenceAdapting to the specific context rather than applying the framework mechanically

The result

A consistent lift, not a single lucky run.

Across the three platforms, the framework moved output from competent to decision-ready. The averages, on the same one-to-ten scale:

MeasureScore
AI baseline average~6.1 / 10
GreenSquare framework average~8.2 / 10
Framework lift against baseline~35 to 45%

The lift matters less than the reversal. A higher score on a launch plan is useful. Catching that the launch was the wrong question is the point.

Caveats

Where this test is honest about its limits.

  • The company is fictional. FreshTaste Foods does not exist. The brief is constructed to be hard. The reversal is a real result on that brief, reproduced independently on all three platforms.
  • Dual-role, not live interview. The model played both consultant and client. This buys repeatability and removes human improvisation, at the cost of some realism in the discovery interview.
  • One run used a single-window fallback. The more rigorous protocol runs the consultant and the client in separate windows. Where a platform made that impractical, a single-window fallback was used and noted as a deviation.
  • A later improvement is projected, not re-tested. After the original test, the business-case section was upgraded with a full investment-case workflow (five-year P&L, DCF, sensitivity, scenarios). The projected effect is a framework average near 8.7 and a lift around 50 per cent. This is an estimate based on the original scoring pattern, not a completed re-run, and is presented as such.
