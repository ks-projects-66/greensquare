Methodology

How the benchmark was run.

The headline is the reversal. This page explains the test behind it, including what it does and does not prove.

← Back to the evidence

The design

Matched inputs, two conditions.

The test compared a baseline response against a GreenSquare framework response. Each run used the same fictional business case. The baseline answered the case directly. The framework run followed the GreenSquare decision structure before producing the brief.

  • Platforms. Claude, ChatGPT and Gemini.
  • Inputs. One fictional business case, used consistently across all runs.
  • Scoring. Outputs were scored against the same twelve-dimension rubric.
  • Purpose. The test asks whether better structure produces a more decision-ready answer.

The test case

The case is a Sydney food manufacturer, FreshTaste Foods, deciding whether to launch a ready-to-drink functional beverage. The brief includes high facility use, limited cash after recent investment, debt covenants, a competitor entering the category and two existing business units with problems of their own.

What changed

The input did not change. The structure did. The framework forced the decision to be tested before the recommendation was written. That is why the answer moved from launch planning to a diagnostic first.

The rubric

Twelve dimensions, scored one to ten.

Every output was scored against the same instrument. The five categories on the evidence page are a plain-language summary of these twelve.

The result

A consistent lift, with the reversal doing the real work.

Across the tested platforms, the framework moved output from competent to decision-ready. The averages, on the same one-to-ten scale:

MeasureScore
AI baseline average~6.1 / 10
GreenSquare framework average~8.2 / 10
Framework lift against baseline~35 to 45%

The lift matters less than the reversal. A better launch plan is useful. Catching that the launch was the wrong question is the point.

Caveats

Where this test is honest about its limits.

  • The company is fictional. FreshTaste Foods does not exist. The case was built to test a hard decision.
  • Benchmark, not field trial. This tests output quality in a controlled setting. It does not replace judgment in a live decision.
  • Small sample. The evidence is useful, but not exhaustive. It shows a strong pattern, not statistical proof.
  • Scoring involves judgment. The same rubric was used across outputs, but scoring quality remains partly evaluative.
